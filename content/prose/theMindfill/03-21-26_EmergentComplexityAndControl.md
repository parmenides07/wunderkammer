## High Skill Ceiling, Low Skill Floor
- Something I've always been fascinated with is simple building blocks that come together to form complex systems or products. A concept that I've tried applying to as much as I could is the idea of a subject having a low skill floor and high skill ceiling. For those unfamiliar, this term originates from the videogame community. Low skill floor means a game is very easy to pick up, typically quite simple. But, a high skill ceiling means the game has very complex mechanics that can be mastered.

### A Flavor
- I've been very interested in a specific flavor of this "low skill floor, high skill ceiling" idea: simple building blocks that can be put together in complex ways. 
- I must draw the distinction between this and other flavors. Other flavors may have very deep yet one dimensional mechanics. 
    - For example Geometry Dash is incredibly easy to pick up, just a game of pressing the space bar and timing properly, but it can become incredibly complex and require frame-perfect clicks. 
    - This flavor does not quite interest me as much as that of Minecraft where you are given these singular blocks and can assemble them in a multitude of ways.

### Foe Obliteration
- Not so similarly but kinda similarly, Noita is another great example of a game that contains the flavor I desire. In this game you can create wands by stringing together spells. 
    - There are a large variety of spells, each quite unassuming on their lonesome. However, together, you can form absolutely insane creations. 
    - I'll give you an example. Let's pick out some simple spells: the double spell, which casts the next two spells in the wand order at once; the spark bolt, a slow and weak but far reaching projectile; and the chainsaw, a fast, but mana draining melee spell. 
        - On their own, these spells are essentially useless, the spark bolt is the spell you start out with and work quickly to dispose of, the chainsaw is useless unless you're at melee range which is rarely, and the double spell needs 2 spells to even function. 
        - However, if you arrange these spells together in a specific orientation their various behaviors will work together. The double spell will cast the spark bolt and the chainsaw at once, the chainsaw will provide its speed and the spark bolt will provide its range, effectively allowing you to spray a continuous blast of spark bolts, covering the screen with purple radiant projectiles and obliterating any foe that lies in your path.

## Emergent Complexity
- Okay, so what? Well, it was just recently when I discovered the name of this ideology: emergence and emergent complexity. I attribute that to the wonderful video that made its way onto my recommended: [Emergent Complexity - Emergent Garden](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0HqUYpGQIfs&t=777s). Emergent complexity is when seemingly simple, lackluster components come together to form complex systems. Atoms and elements, code and software, cells and life. Emergent complexity surrounds us.

## Emergent Systems for Optimal Control
- Something half baked that I also want to explore is the distinction between emergent systems and control. Lets begin with the idea of control and set the context for any kind of creation. Is it true that the lower we go in abstraction, the more control over something we have? Its easiest to think of this in terms of programming, if you want even more control than python you would perhaps turn to C, a lower level language where you'd be tasked with managing memory among many other added responsibilities. Your level of control has increased but so has your responsibility and the skill floor, so to say. Even more control? You'd turn to assembly but your control and responsibilities have considerably increased. 
- All of these are still emergent systems, each composed of simple building blocks, but what has changed is the skill floor, or the number of these parts required to create something. If this example didn't make sense I implore you to think about legos. If you were given half assembled sets and told to build whatever you want, it'd be quite difficult. Maybe you'd be able to frankenstein those parts together and create something interesting but it would likely be very difficult, because of the lack of control. This lack of control too added friction but in the form of limitation rather than responsibility, it made you more limited in the execution of your vision because you are no longer working with simple building blocks. So my conclusion is this, emergent systems are vital to understand so you can determine which layer of a subject you must work at to achieve the most control. 
- You must choose one that provides a balance of the simplest basic blocks, requires the least number of blocks, forms the most powerful systems from those blocks, and provides the least friction in the usage of those blocks. 
- Its worth mentioning as a note that the scope of control must also be understood, where exactly do you want control, what do you need and not need. For we are mere mortals and there is only so far we can go before it becomes inefficient for executing that which we want to execute.

### Our Mortal Limitations and AI's Part in Exacting Our Vision
- My thoughts all this time were that more control means a more accurate rendition of your vision, whether this be for programming, art, or even carpentry. But to what end? Our largest limiting factor is time, you don't have the time to learn how to cut down a tree and process wood if all you want to do is make a cutting board. You don't have the time to build your own CPU and GPU and write your own operating system so that you can build a digital painting software. The next limiting factor is this responsibility that I spoke of. 
- As humans we only have so much intellect and ability, to be given all this responsibility requires a greater level of skill. But I have begun to see abstraction as a gift, less so a limitation. The ideal tool would be one that gives you the reigns and corrects the errors you leave in your wake, and one that steers you in the direction that is most productive. I don't believe our vision is always the best for achieving the creation we want to achieve. 
- I ask then, is it possible that the usage of a tool can actually give more control rather than less, by getting rid of that friction and picking up behind you? This is what I hope AI turns into: the ultimate tool to exacting your vision. It allows your true vision to come forth free from the misdirection of your conscious and mortal shortcomings.
