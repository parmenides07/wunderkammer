## Process Vs Result
- Something I've been going back and forth on for a while is whether to focus on the process of learning or the result of actually shipping something. And I think I've landed somewhere.

### Boxland
- This was an issue I encountered frequently in my practice of art. I've always been putting off creating the cool stories and worlds for one day in the future "when I'm good enough". That day will never come. For years I've been stuck drawing boxes and doing studies not even trying my hand at what I originally started art for. 

- This mentality of just wanting to learn and grind till I'm insane at something conflicted with my newfound life philosophy of creation and output. Programming was the perfect place for me to see the error of my ways. 

- The way I've always operated is that I want to learn, and learn deeply, so that one day I can create something great. But why not just try to create great things today and learn a ton in that process? Not only does that get you to the thing faster, I'd argue it actually gets you to a deeper level of understanding too.

### Pursuit of Something Great
- Neccesity is the mother of education. When you're trying to make something as good as it possibly can be, you are forced to learn what you need to learn to get it there. In your pusuit of striving for greatness, you will be forced to learn even more and more to make it as good as it can be. Chasing perfection will lead you down new roads if not at least strengthen the ones you've already crossed. The context of application makes the learning stick in a way that studying for the sake of studying just doesn't. You're not learning something because it was in a textbook. You're learning it because your program is 5% less effecient without. That's a completely different kind of knowing.
- I think about this in terms of what Steve Jobs understood about hardware and software. For the software to be the best it could be, you have to know the hardware to its fullest, they have to work in tune. Here, the hardware is your knowledge and concepts and the software is the execution of your vision. You can't fake one to get the other. You must strengthen and hone your knowledge and concepts and the ability to apply those to exact that vision. So if you set your sights high and stay determined, you will learn more trying to reach that goal than you ever would have otherwise.
    - This brings up a subpoint I wanted to make: you must have incredible resolve and dedication to what you are building. So much so that you don't give up. You must have a hunger to bring your vision to life and to stop at nothing short of perfection. Only then will you learn all that there is to learn. For me, I've discovered that when I feel like giving up I cut out aspects of the project and try to create an MVP without them and once I get to that point I can't help but bring back the things I cut for sake of achieving something I really am proud of. 
    
- The idea is that when striving for perfection, even in the process of adding those last few details and polishing it and pushing it further to perfection, you are learning horizontally, picking up things you never would have otherwise. Beyond this you are honing that which you know, repition of the application of concepts solidifies the concepts. 

-  A byproduct of focusing on the creation of something rather than endless learning is that it aligns with the principles of SMART objectives: Creating objectives that are specific, measurable, achieveable, relavant, and time bound. 

### I've Always Had the Same Idealogy
- I find it interesting that I've always had the same idealogy of just wanting too perfect and hone even if it means I'll never actually finish something. Before I wanted to practice and hone my craft so I could one day make something. Now, I learn while working on something, perfecting and honing and stopping at nothing short of attainable greatness and complete innovation. 
- I really never saw the point in making stuff to make stuff, before I'd prefer to just practice and now I prefer to set my sights higher, even if the cost of that is never finishing it.
  - If I truly believe in the idea and its potential, I will do everything in my power to exact it.
